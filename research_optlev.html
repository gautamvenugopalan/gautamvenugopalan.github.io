Optical levitation and search for modifications to Newtonian gravity

While General Relativity (GR) is the most complete description of gravity we have, the effort to unify it with quantum mechanics is one of the open problems in physics today. One avenue to search for clues is to study GR's predecessor, Newtonian gravity - while it has been verified to exquisite precision at length scales larger than $\approx 1\,\mathrm{mm}$, constraints on its accuracy are much weaker at smaller length scales. A popular parametrization of deviations from Newtonian gravity takes the form $V(r) = -\frac{GMm}{r}(1+\alpha e^{-\lambda/r})$, with $\alpha$ quantifying the strength of the deviation, and $\lambda$ encoding a characteristic length scale at which the deviation becomes significant. The graphic below shows the current best limits on this parametrization, determined using a variety of experimental techniques.

At length scales $\lesssim 10\,\mathrm{\mu m}$, there is considerable room to improve the constraints on these parameters. For instance, the gravitational force between two objects separated by $1\,\mathrm{\mu m}$ could be a million times stronger than predicted by Newtonian gravity - we just haven't looked! Optical levitation offers an excellent platform to probe gravity at such length scales. Building on the pioneering work of Arthur Ashkin, we are able to trap $\sim 8\,\mathrm{\mu m}$ sized silica microspheres in a tightly focused laser beam. Under active feedback, the levitated trapped spheres are sufficiently isolated from their environment, such that they can be used as force sensors with $\sim 10\,\mathrm{aN/\sqrt{Hz}}$ sensitivity. We can then bring a "test-mass" close (within $\approx 5\,\mathrm{\mu m}$) to the levitated trapped sphere, and measure the gravitational force between them. I am presently working on improving the sensitivity of such a system in order to probe some of the unexplored $\alpha - \lambda$ parameter space.
