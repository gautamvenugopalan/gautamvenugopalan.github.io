Experimental interests

In trying to make ever more precise measurements to answer unresolved questions in physics, careful engineering of the mechanics and electronics of an experiment are key to overcoming technical limitations. Recent advances in computational techniques offer another valuable tool, enabling the implementation of more sophisticated feedback control, and noise subtraction from the measurement channel. My interests and experience as an experimental physicist are in developing better instrumentation and data analysis pipelines, thereby improving the sensitivity of experiments both on the tabletop, and at the scale of experiments such as LIGO.

I spent my graduate school career commissioning the LIGO interferometers in what was the era of first direct detection of gravitational waves and multi-messenger astronomy. Much of my time was spent at the 40 m prototype interferometer at Caltech, where new ideas for improving the sensitivity of the full-sized 4 km interferometers are tested out.

Now, I continue to work on experiments testing gravity, but at the table-top scale. Optical levitation, and Mössbauer spectroscopy, are two techniques with which we can search for hypothetical new forces or fields which have a short ($\lesssim 10\,\mathrm{\mu m}$) interaction range. There are an abundance of theories which postulate the existence of such hypothetical new forces or fields modifying gravity, but technical challenges have limited the precision with which we can search for them at relevant length scales. The hope is that such searches will throw up new clues in the quest to unify the two pillars of 20th century physics, General Relativity and Quantum Mechanics.
